Lesson 12 — Subheadings <h2>–<h6>

← Back to Curriculum ← Previous Lesson Next Lesson →
Estimated time: 25–35 minutes · Learn how subheadings organize content below <h1>

Learning Goals

  • Understand what <h2>–<h6> elements are for.
  • Learn how subheadings create structure under the main heading.
  • Use them properly for accessibility and SEO.

Part 1 — What are Subheadings?

Subheadings <h2>–<h6> divide content into logical sections. <h2> is used for main sections under <h1>, then <h3> for subsections, and so on down to <h6>.

Rule: Think of it like a book: <h1> is the title, <h2> are chapter titles, <h3> are subsections inside chapters.

Part 2 — Example

<body>
  <h1>My Travel Blog</h1>

  <h2>Europe</h2>
    <h3>France</h3>
    <p>I loved visiting Paris.</p>
    <h3>Italy</h3>
    <p>Rome and Venice were amazing.</p>

  <h2>Asia</h2>
    <h3>Japan</h3>
    <p>Tokyo was full of energy.</p>
</body>

Part 3 — Best Practices

  • Use headings in order: <h1> → <h2> → <h3>, etc.
  • Don’t skip levels (avoid jumping from <h1> to <h4>).
  • Keep headings descriptive and short.
  • Use CSS for styling, not heading level misuse.

Part 4 — Common Mistakes

  • ❌ Using headings just to make text bigger/bolder.
  • ❌ Skipping levels (e.g., <h1> → <h4>).
  • ❌ Multiple <h1> headings but no <h2> structure.

Quick Quiz

  1. Which heading level comes after <h2>?
  2. True/False: You should skip from <h1> directly to <h4>.
  3. Why are subheadings important for accessibility?
Sample Answers
  • <h3>.
  • False — avoid skipping levels.
  • They give screen readers and users a clear outline of page structure.

Mini Project — Outline a Page

Create a page with:

  • A <h1> title.
  • Two <h2> sections (e.g., “About Me” and “Projects”).
  • Inside each <h2>, at least one <h3> subsection.
